## Algorithms and Interview Prep

Sites focused on data structures, algorithms, and technical interview preparation.

- [LeetCode](https://leetcode.com) - The go-to platform for interview prep, with thousands of problems ranging from easy to hard. Companies practically borrow from its problem bank when interviewing you.

- [HackerRank](https://www.hackerrank.com) - Coding challenges, competitions, and hiring contests all in one place. Covers algorithms, databases, AI, and more — many companies screen candidates here.

- [GeeksforGeeks](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org) 🇮🇳 - Part tutorial site, part problem bank, with detailed explanations and real interview experiences. Great when you need to understand the *why* behind a solution.

- [HackerEarth](https://www.hackerearth.com) 🇮🇳 - Blends coding practice with actual hiring challenges. Problems span multiple difficulty levels and they run hackathons too.

- [InterviewBit](https://www.interviewbit.com) 🇮🇳 - Curated problems that mirror real interview questions from top tech companies. Follows a structured path and tracks your progress along the way.

- [NeetCode](https://neetcode.io) - A LeetCode-style platform built specifically for interview prep. Problem sets are organized by company and difficulty so you know exactly what to study.

- [AlgoMonster](https://algo.monster) - A structured curriculum that focuses on pattern recognition rather than grinding random problems. Great if you want to learn algorithms systematically.

- [CodeSignal](https://codesignal.com) - Gamified coding assessments that companies actually use to screen candidates. Good way to benchmark yourself before the real thing.

- [Interview Cake](https://www.interviewcake.com) - Interview prep with unusually thorough explanations that walk you through the entire thought process. Covers algorithms, data structures, and system design.

- [Codility](https://www.codility.com) 🇵🇱 - Used by companies to run coding assessments, but their training lessons are solid practice too. Work through those before attempting challenges.

- [TryExponent](https://www.tryexponent.com) - Practice problems paired with mock interviews to help you get comfortable under pressure. Focused on landing jobs at top tech companies.

- [Edabit](https://edabit.com) - Bite-sized challenges that teach programming concepts one step at a time. Ideal warm-up before diving into the harder platforms.

- [CodingBat](https://codingbat.com) - Straightforward Java and Python problems focused on basic logic and recursion. Created by Nick Partridge at UC Berkeley for absolute beginners.

- [Daily Coding Problem](https://dailycodingproblem.com) - Sends one interview-style problem to your inbox every day. Consistent daily practice without having to hunt for problems.

- [Learneroo](https://www.learneroo.com) - Algorithms practice with structured learning paths for interview prep. Follow the paths for a guided experience.

- [Codeintuition](https://codeintuition.com) - Practice platform covering various programming topics across different skill levels. Use it alongside LeetCode for topic-specific drilling.

- [CoderByte](https://coderbyte.com) - Quick coding challenges that mimic interview questions. Good for squeezing in practice between other study sessions.

- [NaukriCode360](https://code360.naukri.com) 🇮🇳 - Popular in India for placement preparation, with problems, articles, and interview experiences. Good companion to LeetCode for Indian job seekers.

- [Codemia](https://codemia.io) - AI-powered system design practice with visualizations and peer mock interviews. Built for engineers targeting FAANG-level system design rounds.

## Competitive Programming

Platforms that host regular contests and competitions for algorithmic problem-solving.

- [CodeForces](https://codeforces.com) 🇷🇺 - The heavyweight of competitive programming, hosting regular contests with tough algorithmic problems. Known for its fast-paced rounds and strong community.

- [CodeChef](https://www.codechef.com) 🇮🇳 - Monthly contests with a large problem library and a solid rating system. Supports multiple languages and runs longer challenges that let you really dig into problems.

- [SPOJ](https://www.spoj.com) 🇵🇱 - Sphere Online Judge has a massive archive of problems spanning every category you can think of. Great when you want to grind through a huge variety of challenges.

- [AtCoder](https://atcoder.jp) 🇯🇵 - Japan's most popular competitive programming platform, known for well-structured contests with high-quality problems. The Beginner Contest is a great entry point.

- [Kattis](https://open.kattis.com) 🇸🇪 - A problem archive used in actual programming competitions. Filter by difficulty score to find problems at your level.

- [UVa OnlineJudge](https://onlinejudge.org) 🇪🇸 - The classic archive from the University of Valladolid with over 4,000 problems. Use uHunt to track your progress by topic.

- [DMOJ](https://dmoj.ca) 🇨🇦 - An open-source judge that hosts contests and supports multiple languages. Particularly popular for Canadian contest preparation.

- [Timus OnlineJudge](https://acm.timus.ru) 🇷🇺 - Russian contest archive with problems from Timus and ACM finals. The "Timus Top 50" is a good place to start.

- [Aizu OnlineJudge](https://onlinejudge.u-aizu.ac.jp/home) 🇯🇵 - Japanese practice platform from Aizu University with problems from local programming contests. The Course section offers structured learning.

- [CSAcademy](https://csacademy.com/) - Interactive algorithm learning with courses and an intuitive visual editor. Great for understanding difficult algorithms visually.

- [E-Olymp](https://e-olymp.com) 🇦🇿 - Educational platform with problems and contests used by schools and universities. Good for structured training.

- [LightOJ](https://lightoj.com) 🇧🇩 - A diverse problem collection known for its number theory problems. Good math practice alongside regular algorithms.

- [AlgoLeague](https://algoleague.com) 🇹🇷 - Regular algorithm competitions with a focus on live contest participation. Test your skills under time pressure.

- [Toph](https://toph.co) 🇧🇩 - Bangladeshi platform with contests and practice problems in multiple languages. Good for regional contest exposure.

- [TLXTOKI](https://tlx.toki.id) 🇮🇩 - Indonesian programming contest platform hosting national contests and practice problems. The primary platform for Indonesia's programming scene.

- [ACMHDU](https://acm.hdu.edu.cn) 🇨🇳 - Chinese online judge from Hangzhou Dianzi University with a large problem set. Search by algorithm topic in the problem index.

- [CodeAbbey](https://codeabbey.com) 🇷🇺 - Problems start easy and ramp up gently, making it a welcoming place for new programmers. Good starting point before tackling harder platforms.

- [KEP](https://kep.uz) 🇺🇿 - Uzbek platform with algorithmic problems and contests for the Central Asian community. Regional contest sets you won't find on Western platforms.

- [Kilorova](https://kilorova.ro) 🇷🇴 - Romanian contest site with problems pulled from local competitions. Good exposure to Eastern European problem styles.

- [Serious-OJ](https://serious-oj.com) - Exactly what it says: hard problems for people who find CodeForces too easy. Come back once you're comfortable with Div 1 contests.

- [Eldarverse](https://eldarverse.com) - Newer platform with a clean editor and real-time feedback. Good supplementary practice when you've exhausted the bigger sites.

- [andgein.ru](https://2026.andgein.ru/?lang=en) 🇷🇺 - Russian contest site with a unique annual New Year's competition. The problems have a flavor you won't find on Western platforms.

## Full-Stack and Web Development

Interactive courses and tutorials for building web development skills.

- [FreeCodeCamp](https://www.freecodecamp.org) - A completely free full-stack curriculum with coding challenges and projects to build your portfolio. Self-paced and covers everything from HTML to data science.

- [W3Schools](https://www.w3schools.com) 🇳🇴 - The classic reference for web development, covering HTML, CSS, JavaScript, SQL, and more. Best used as a quick reference rather than a deep dive.

- [Codecademy](https://www.codecademy.com) - Hands-on lessons with instant feedback in a variety of languages. Good for getting syntax down fast when learning something new.

- [The Odin Project](https://www.theodinproject.com) - A free, project-focused full-stack curriculum covering HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and Ruby. About as close to a bootcamp as you can get without paying.

- [Boot.dev](https://boot.dev) - Backend-focused learning path with courses in Python and Go. Build real backend skills through projects.

- [NewtonSchool](https://www.newtonschool.com) 🇮🇳 - Coding bootcamp and practice platform popular in India. Full-stack courses paired with placement preparation.

- [SoloLearn](https://www.sololearn.com) - Bite-sized coding lessons designed for mobile. Great for learning on the go during commutes or breaks.

- [Codedex](https://codedex.io) - Fun, bite-sized challenges for beginners that make learning to code feel less like studying. Good entry point for complete newcomers.

- [CodeCrafters](https://codecrafters.io) - You build real tools like Git and Docker from scratch, which gives you a deep understanding of how they work. Great for engineers who want to go beyond surface-level knowledge.

## Frontend Development

Frontend-specific challenges and practice for honing UI and design skills.

- [FrontendMentor](https://www.frontendmentor.io) 🇬🇧 - Real-world design-to-code challenges at increasing difficulty levels. Build something from scratch, then compare your solution with others.

- [CodePen](https://codepen.io) - A playground for experimenting with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Great for prototyping ideas and building a collection of frontend experiments.

- [GreatFrontend](https://www.greatfrontend.com) - Frontend interview prep covering JavaScript, React, CSS, and system design. Problems are tagged by company so you can target specific employers.

- [CSSBattle](https://cssbattle.dev) 🇮🇳 - You recreate designs using nothing but CSS. Surprisingly addictive way to level up your CSS skills.

- [FireCode](https://firecode.io) - Frontend-focused coding practice with JavaScript and React. Good for targeted frontend interview preparation.

- [The Coding Train](https://thecodingtrain.com) - Daniel Shiffman's creative coding tutorials, especially for p5.js and generative art. Makes programming feel playful and accessible.

## Language and Niche Practice

Platforms focused on specific languages, domains, or creative coding approaches.

- [CodeWars](https://www.codewars.com) 🇬🇧 - Solve kata-ranked challenges in dozens of languages, leveling up as you go. Great for learning new languages through structured repetition.

- [Advent of Code](https://adventofcode.com) - An annual December event with 25 daily puzzles that ramp from cozy to brutal. Has a devoted following for good reason.

- [Exercism](https://exercism.org) - Practice with mentor feedback on your solutions in over 65 languages. The code reviews alone are worth it.

- [CodinGame](https://www.codingame.com) 🇫🇷 - You write code to control game characters and solve puzzles. A fun way to pick up new languages without it feeling like work.

- [ProjectEuler](https://projecteuler.net) 🇬🇧 - Math-heavy programming problems that require insight as much as coding skill. Perfect if you enjoy the intersection of math and code.

- [CodeGym](https://codegym.cc) - A gamified Java course that takes you from complete beginner to competent. Best for Java learners who want a structured path.

- [CheckIO](https://checkio.org) - Python challenges with a gaming twist — solve puzzles to unlock more puzzles and earn achievements.

- [Rosalind](https://rosalind.info) - Bioinformatics problems working with DNA, RNA, and genetics. A niche pick for programmers interested in computational biology.

- [PyBites](https://pybitesplatform.com/) - Python practice with real-world coding challenges. The "100 Days of Code" is a solid way to build consistency.

- [4clojure](https://4clojure.oxal.org/) - Interactive Clojure problems for learning functional programming. Good entry point if you're curious about Clojure.

- [SQLZoo](https://sqlzoo.net) 🇬🇧 - Interactive SQL tutorials and exercises at every level. Work through all of them before attempting interview questions.

- [PythonCode.club](https://pythoncode.club) - Python problems sorted by topic, letting you drill specific areas like list comprehensions or the standard library. Good supplementary practice.

- [Highload.fun](https://highload.fun) - Challenges focused on concurrency, memory, and optimization. A rare niche that backend engineers will appreciate.

- [Hackattic](https://hackattic.com) - Practical hacking problems covering web exploits, crypto, and reverse engineering. Less hand-holding than other platforms, more real figuring-things-out.

- [SupeCoder](https://supecoder.dev) - No-frills coding practice with contests and instant feedback. Nothing fancy, but it works.

- [CodeAny](https://codeany.me) - Minimalist problem-solving — no distracting features, just pick a problem and write code. Good for quick warm-up sessions.

- [RepoVive](https://repovive.com) - Practice by contributing to real open-source repos. Less about algorithms, more about reading and working with actual codebases.

- [CodenQuest](https://codenquest.com) - Gamified coding with quests and achievements that get harder as you progress. Makes practice feel less like a chore.

- [Vim Adventures](https://vim-adventures.com) - Learn Vim navigation through an adventure game. Play through the levels and you'll pick up Vim commands naturally.

## Data Science and Machine Learning

- [Kaggle](https://www.kaggle.com) - The heart of the data science community, with competitions, datasets, and courses all in one place. Start with the "Getting Started" competitions before jumping into the real ones.

- [StrataScratch](https://www.stratascratch.com) - Real interview questions from actual data science companies. Filter by company to practice exactly what you'll be asked.

## CTF and Security Platforms

CTF (Capture The Flag) sites are used for practicing cybersecurity skills and solving challenges.

- [TryHackMe](https://tryhackme.com) 🇬🇧 - Guided hacking labs that walk you through rooms step by step. The "Pre Security" path is a great on-ramp if you're new to security.

- [HackTheBox](https://www.hackthebox.eu) - Challenging machines and CTF-style labs for more experienced security folks. Start with the easy machines and work your way up.

- [PicoCTF](https://picoctf.org) - A beginner-friendly CTF competition pitched at high school and college students. Great entry point if you've never done a CTF before.

- [OverTheWire](https://overthewire.org) - A series of wargames that start from absolute basics and ramp up to advanced security concepts. Complete Bandit before moving to the harder ones.

- [CTFLearn](https://ctflearn.com) - Community-driven CTF practice with problems across all categories. Sort by difficulty and progress at your own pace.

- [CryptoHack](https://cryptohack.org) 🇦🇺 - Learn modern cryptography through hands-on challenges. Work through the Introduction section first to build a foundation.

- [W3Challs](https://w3challs.com) 🇫🇷 - Security challenges covering web, system, and network exploits across multiple categories. Good for well-rounded security practice.
